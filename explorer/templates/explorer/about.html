The JHB at 50
A computational reflection on fifty years of the Journal of the History of Biology.

In his editorial foreward to the first issue of the Journal of the History of Biology, Everett Mendelsohn made the following remarks.

Contemporary scholarship in the history of science makes changed demands upon the author; these are demands for methodological awareness and realization that other fields of historical study have brought new sophistication to the writing of history.
Everett Mendelsohn, JHB Volume 1, Issue 1, Page iii

Mendelsohn's admonition to go beyond the "simple narrative", and bring to bear the full battery of critical and comparative lenses afforded by historical scholarship, did not go unheeded. Today, the field of history of biology is a bustling intersection of scholarly approaches, weaving together perspectives from sociology, economics, and environmental studies into a rich collection of historiographic theories, models, and methods.

Historians of science are continuing that tradition of methodological awareness in the digital age. As the history of science moves online, historians are experimenting with innovative ways of organizing and communicating the "changing concepts and altered methods of experiment and observation" that characterize scientific activity.

This online exhibit turns a few of those digital methods back onto the discipline of the history of science itself.

This project is an experiment in creating new views onto our own scholarly productions and the development of our field. The techniques that we have employed range from the trivially simple—like placing points on a map—to the complex—using Bayesian machine-learning techniques to model latent themes across the thousands of pages of articles published in JHB over the past fifty years.

The goal of this interactive exhibit is to prompt a conversation about the structure and presentation of our collective knowledge in the digital domain.

Our sources are online, our notes are increasingly digital, and our books and articles are increasingly consumed in electronic form. What are the opportunities, challenges, and imaginations for historical scholarship in the age of the internet?

Structure of the Project

The nucleus of this online exhibit are the digized contents of the Journal of the History of Biology itself, the bulk of which were graciously provided by the folks at JSTOR. Having access to these complete documents means that we have been able to analyze them on a page-by-page basis. The main components of our analysis are described briefly below, and more details can be found here.

The Topic Model

Topic modeling is a technique developed to optimize internet search engines by discovering or inferring latent concepts or topics scattered across extremely large collections of digital texts. In the last decade, topic modeling has become popular in Digital Humanities as a discovery tool, as described in this special issue of Journal of Digital Humanities.

We modeled the thematic content of each page in JHB, and used the co-occurrence of topics on individual pages to explore how themes are related over time. We've used the topic model in other ways, too: finding related authors and documents, and looking at the thematic context of organisms that occur in JHB.

Organisms: Named Entity Recognition

Named Entity Recognition (NER) is a machine-learning technique for identify passages of text that refer to things like people, places, and dates. We used NER to identify references to particular taxa in JHB, and then cross-referenced them with the NCBI Taxonomy database. The result is an intruiging picture of how historians have discussed individual taxa and major taxonomic divisions over time.
